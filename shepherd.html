Questions come to mind about the purpose, contents and style of the book

If the author was concerned with presenting information essential to the salvation of one's eternal soul, why is the redemptive work of Jesus allegorized throughout the entire book? Why not simply present the facts about Jesus' message and ministry and let the Holy Spirit do the work of calling the believer? It seems the purpose of the text is long-form persuation and indoctrination, not salvation by believing in the redemptive work of Jesus in history.

Why is the Son not named? Why is the name "Jesus" absent from the entire text? There is an emphasis on knowing the name of the Son of God, as if his name is a mystery which, once revealed to the initiate, the power of the name itself brings the initiate into the secret family of God. I imagine early Christianity (associated with the sect that circulated this text) to be a mystery religion where the proselyte is initated by baptism and then told the secret, powerful name of the Son of God. They must then continue in the faith by attending communion (a common practice of mystery cults in those days) and bringing regular tithes to maintain their position in God's Holy Church.

The New Testament canon and the essential teachings of Christianity come down to us through history from the early Orthodox/Catholic church. The Shepherd of Hermas was once early considered "canon" by the Church. This early text is clearly story-telling and myth-making. If that's the case with The Shepherd, it's also true of the rest of the New Testament.
